Abstract 
 
Dyslexia, dyscalculia, dysgraphia, non -verbal learning disabilities, and auditory 
processing disorder can easily remain unidentified in the school environment, thus 
leading to delayed interventions for the learners. The proposed project involves the use 
of an AI-based web system known as Taranga to aid in the early screening, analysis, and

language processing algorithms, and multi -label machine learning models to point out

based on these outcomes. Relying on intelligent screening methods as well as systematic 
student progress tracking strategie s, "Taranga" hopes to help teache rs, parents, and 
institutions make data-driven decisions related to a student’s inclusive and personalized 
education way. This interim report presents progress up to system design and planning 
stages.

1. Introduction 
1.1 Current Scenario 
A great majority of the Learning disabilities such as Dyslexia, Dyscalculia, Dysgraphia, 
and so on, remain unidentified within the school setup. The teachers base the 
identification on the observation and performance level within the classroom, which takes 
time. Within most learning institutions, there is a constraint on the resources needed to 
aid in the identification and monitoring of the potential Learning disability, if any, within 
the individual. 
There is a  great opportunity to develop intelligent systems that assist teachers in 
identifying learning difficulties early and recommending appropriate interventions with the 
rapid increase of AI in education sector. However, most existing systems either focus on 
a single learning difficulty or provide support tools without structured screening and 
progress tracking. 
 
1.2 Problem Statement 
Schools lack an integrated, AI-powered system that supports early screening of multiple 
learning disabilities that analyses and provides personalized intervention strategies while 
tracking student progress over time. The absence of such systems leads to delayed 
identification of learning difficulties and ineffective interventions. 
 
1.3 Project as a Solution 
The proposed project, Taranga, addresses this problem by providing an AI -based 
learning disability screening and intervention recommendation system. The system 
enables teachers to conduct adaptive screening questionnaires and submit qualitative 
observations, which are analysed using machine learning and natural language 
processing techniques. Based on the results, the system recommends personalized 
intervention activities for students and continuously tracks their progress through 
interactive practice activities and dashboards. \n\n--- PAGE 11 ---\nCS6P05N1 | FYP INTERIM REPORT

1.4 Aim and Objectives 
1.4.1 Aim 
The aim of this project is to design and develop an AI -powered web -based system to 
facilitate the early detection of learning disabilities in students and provide them with 
interventions based on progress tracking. 
1.4.2 Objectives

2.5 Comparison Analysis of Similar Projects  
This section analyses the five reviewed systems and identifies the functional and research 
gaps that Taranga aims to address. 
2.5.1 Key Patterns Observed in Existing Systems 
There is a clear pattern across the five systems that most specialize in one part of the 
support pipeline, but do not provide an integrated solution. 
• Dytective, Lexplore, and Cognoa  primarily focus on screening/assessment, but 
provide limited or no structured intervention planning and do not track student 
improvement over time in an educational workflow. 
• Read&Write provides assistive support for reading/writing, but it does not perform 
screening, AI-based prediction, or structured progress analytics. 
• ClassDojo supports monitoring and parent communication, but it does not address 
LD screening or AI-driven educational recommendations. 
In summary, current systems tend to fall into two categories: 
• AI screening tools without interventions/tracking (Dytective, Lexplore, Cognoa) 
• Support/management tools without AI screening (Read&Write, ClassDojo) 
Taranga stands as a solution that bridges this gap by offering screening + intervention + 
progress tracking in one workflow. 
 
2.5.2 Screening Scope and Multi-Condition Coverage 
A major limitation in the reviewed systems is narrow screening scope: 
• Dytective mainly addresses dyslexia. 
• Lexplore focuses on reading and literacy behaviour, not broader LD categories. 
• Cognoa focuses on autism spectrum disorder and is not designed for classroom 
intervention workflows. 
 \n\n--- PAGE 23 ---\nCS6P05N1 | FYP INTERIM REPORT 
23048503 | Sanskriti Agrahari  14 
This creates a gap for schools where students may experience overlapping difficulties. 
Taranga addresses this limitation through a multi-label approach, where the system can 
flag multiple LD indicators at once. This is more realistic in educational contexts where 
learning challenges rarely occur in isolation. 
 
2.5.3 Data Types Used: Structured vs. Real Classroom Evidence 
Most existing screening systems rely on a limited form of input: 
• Dytective largely uses performance patterns in assessment tasks. 
• Lexplore relies on physiological eye-tracking signals. 
• Cognoa relies heavily on parent input. 
A key gap is the limited use of teacher-generated qualitative observations, even though 
teachers observe learning behaviour daily. Taranga explicitly includes teacher 
observation analysis using NLP , which strengthens classroom relevance and creates a 
richer evidence base. This also improves explainability because the system can show 
what behavioural indicators contributed to the screening output. 
 
2.5.4 Intervention and Progress Tracking: The Biggest Gap 
The largest gap across reviewed systems is the absence of structured, measurable 
interventions with progress tracking: 
• Dytective does not provide long-term progress tracking. 
• Lexplore focuses on assessment: intervention planning is not the core. 
• Cognoa is clinical and does not provide classroom activity-based intervention. 
• Read&Write supports learning but does not track improvement analytically. 
• ClassDojo tracks behaviour engagement, but not LD-specific skill development. 
Taranga differentiates itself by linking screening outcomes directly to interactive, LD -
specific activities, and tracking progress through measurable indicators such as activity 
scores/accuracy, time taken, attempts, completion rate, consistency/engagement trends 
over time. \n\n--- PAGE 24 ---\nCS6P05N1 | FYP INTERIM REPORT 
23048503 | Sanskriti Agrahari  15 
This makes Taranga not only a screening tool, but also a continuous support system , 
which is more valuable for teachers who need to monitor whether interventions are 
actually working. 
 
2.5.5 Practical Feasibility in Real Schools 
Lexplore demonstrates that advanced assessment systems can be accurate, but the 
requirement for specialized eye-tracking hardware makes it less feasible in low-resource 
environments. Systems that require expensive equipment or specialist support often 
struggle to scale in typical school contexts. 
Taranga’s approach (teacher -led screening + web -based interventions) improves 
feasibility: no specialized hardware,  deployable via a web browse r and accessible for 
schools with basic computer labs.  This makes the proposed system more realistic for 
broader school adoption and long-term use. 
 
2.5.6 Ethical and User-Role Considerations 
A practical and ethical concern in screening systems is how results are communicated. 
Many platforms risk presenting diagnostic outcomes directly to learners. Taranga avoids 
this by applying a teacher-led screening and student -led intervention model , where  
teachers/parents provide screening inputs , view results and reports,  and students only 
access intervention activities and feedback . This improves responsible deployment and 
reduces the risk of stigma, misinterpretation, or labelling. 
 
2.5.7 Overall Positioning of Taranga  
Based on the comparison, Taranga is positioned as a system that combines  adaptive 
screening (teacher /parent-led), multi-label ML prediction,  NLP analysis of teacher 
observations, personalized intervention recommendations,  student-facing practice 
activities, and progress tracking dashboards for teachers/parents. This combination is not 
fully provided by any single reviewed system, showing that Taranga contributes a more 
complete end-to-end workflow for LD support. \n\n--- PAGE 25 ---\nCS6P05N1 | FYP INTERIM REPORT

3. Development Methodology 
3.1 Considered Methodologies 
The following development methodologies were considered for the project: 
 
Waterfall Model 
The Waterfall Model is strictly sequentially oriented, with well-demarcated phases, which 
is very documentation intensive. Though it is appropriate for programs with inherent, well-
pronounced requirements, it is not very flexible if there are changes demanded in the 
process. Since this particular project comprises machine learning tasks, as well as NLP, 
involving continuous experimentation, the Waterfall life cycle was deemed inappropriate 
in this particular scenario. 
 
Agile Methodology 
The Agile methodology is based on the principles of fast development cycles, constant 
user feedback, and fast-changing requirements for the software developed. Though agile 
methodology helps developers stay flexible, it requires constant interaction with diverse 
stakeholders as well as constant sprint planning activities. As the current project included 
limited interaction with the diverse stakeholders and deadlines for assessing the 
stakeholders, it was impractical to implement the agile process entirely. 
 
Spiral Model 
The spiral model is a combination of it erative model with risk analysis. It involves the 
identification of risks strongly and iteratively refining the system. The model has limitations 
where it is deemed documentation heavy considering the time frame and scale of this 
project.

3.2 Selected Methodology 
An Iterative Development Methodology enhanced with Prototyping has been selected for 
this project. This approach involves developing an initial prototype to demonstrate core 
system functionality, followed by multiple iterative cycles that progressively refine system 
features, machine learning models, and user interfaces. Feedback obtained from 
supervisors and results from intermediate testing are incorporated at the end of each 
iteration. 
The selected methodology is appropriate for this project because: 
• Early prototypes help validate screening workflows and user interfaces. 
• Machine learning and NLP models improve through repeated training and 
evaluation. 
• Usability issues can be identified and addressed early. 
• Risks related to model performance and system design can be reduced 
incrementally.

9.2 Considered Methodologies explained 
Below is the detail of all the considered methodologies and they were ultimately not 
chosen for the project. 
• Waterfall Model 
Aspect Description 
Why it was 
considered 
Owing to its systematic and sequential process stages, the Waterfall 
approach has become a classic and well -documented process for the 
Software Development Life Cycle, and it has often been practiced in the 
academic environment as well as formal projects b ecause it involves 
logic and procedural stages such as analysis, design, development, 
testing, and maintenance, suited to projects requiring a considerable 
amount of documents and formal reporting as demanded by the 
academic environment (Tutorialspoint, n.d.). (geeksforgeeks, 2025)  
Strengths High emphasis on documentation, clearly defined deliverables and 
milestones, sequential approach making it easily understandable and 
manageable, and appropriateness for projects with stable and clearly 
defined requirements. 
Why it was 
not chosen 
Iterative testing, model tuning, and refinement are necessary for the 
AI/ML and NLP components of the Taranga system. Iterative model 
improvement and adaptive questionnaire logic are not supported by the 
Waterfall model's strict sequential structure. For A I-driven systems, 
late-stage modifications would necessitate revisiting earlier stages. 
Conclusion The Waterfall model was inappropriate for this research -focused AI 
application due to its lack of flexibility and support for iterative AI 
development, even though it was appropriate for projects with a lot of 
documentation.

9.3 SRS (Introduction) 
1. Introduction 
1.1 Purpose 
The purpose of this document is to outline the functional and nonfunctional requirements 
for Taranga, an AI -powered multidimensional learning difficulty (LD) screening and 
intervention recommendation system. This document defines the system’s expected 
features, constraints, behaviour, and performance characteristics. It provides a 
foundation for design, development, testing, deployment, and maintenance. 
This SRS will serve as a reference for the developer (myself) and my supervisors. 
1.2 Intended Audience 
This document is intended for: 
Supervisors: To assess the scope, feasibility, and completeness of the system. 
Developers (Self): To guide the design and implementation phases. 
Educational Stakeholders:  Teachers, parents, administrators, and special educators 
who may use or evaluate the system’s outputs. 
1.3 Product Scope 
Taranga is designed to help Nepali schools identify learning difficulties early through 
adaptive questionnaires, machine learning predictions, NLP -based teacher observation 
analysis, and personalized intervention suggestions. It enables teachers and parent s to 
conduct screenings, review LD risk levels, and track student progress through interactive 
modules and dashboards. 
The scope of the system includes: 
• Screening for dyslexia, dyscalculia, dysgraphia, APD, NVLD 
• AI-generated LD predictions with explainability 
• NLP processing of teacher notes 
• Interactive intervention exercises \n\n--- PAGE 78 ---\nCS6P05N1 | FYP INTERIM REPORT 
23048503 | Sanskriti Agrahari  69 
• Progress tracking and performance analytics 
• Role-based access for teachers, parents, and administrators 
• Secure data storage and report generation 
 
2 Overall Description 
2.1 Product Perspective 
Taranga is a standalone web -based system developed using FastAPI, ReactJS, and 
SQLAlchemy, integrating AI models for prediction and NLP functionalities. It follows a 
client-server architecture with separate frontend and backend layers. 
The system acts both as: 
• A screening tool for early detection of LD symptoms 
• A support system providing interventions and progress monitoring 
Taranga integrates adaptive logic, multiple AI models, structured data storage, and user-
friendly interfaces to deliver a holistic learning support experience. 
2.2 Product Functions 
At a high level, Taranga will: 
• Register and authenticate users with role-based access 
• Present adaptive questionnaires to students or parents 
• Process teacher observation notes using NLP 
• Generate multi-label LD predictions 
• Produce explainable AI outputs 
• Recommend tailored activities per LD type 
• Provide interactive exercises and track performance 
• Display progress insights through dashboards 
• Generate downloadable reports

9.5 ERD Documentation 
Key Entity Relationships 
• A User may be associated with one or more system roles, while a student entity 
extends user information to store student-specific academic attributes. 
• Each Student has multiple Screening Questions associated to their screening, and 
each question can receive multiple responses, forming a one-to-many relationship 
through the Screening_response entity. 
• Teacher observation notes are linked to both the student and the submitting user, 
enabling qualitative insights to supplement questionnaire-based screening. \n\n--- PAGE 89 ---\nCS6P05N1 | FYP INTERIM REPORT 
23048503 | Sanskriti Agrahari  80 
• Each Screening Result  is generated for a specific student and stores predicted 
learning difficulty indicators along with confidence scores. 
• An Intervention Plan is generated based on a screening result and maps identified 
learning difficulties to suitable activities. 
• Activities are assigned to students through the student_activity entity, allowing 
tracking of assignment status and progression. 
• Activity Performance  records store granular performance metrics for each 
completed activity, supporting longitudinal analysis. 
• Progress Reports  aggregate screening and performance data to present 
summarized insights to teachers and parents. 
 
Business Rules 
• Each user must be registered before accessing system functionality. 
• A student may have multiple screening responses but only one screening result 
per screening cycle. 
• Screening responses must reference a valid student and screening question. 
• Teacher observation notes can only be submitted by authorized users (teachers 
or parents). 
• AI-generated screening results must be linked to a student and include confidence 
scores. 
• Each intervention plan must be associated with exactly one screening result. 
• A student may be assigned multiple activities, but each activity assignment must 
reference a valid activity. 
• Activity performance records must only exist for assigned student activities. 
• Progress reports must reference an existing student and authorized user. 
• Deleting a student record is restricted if dependent screening, intervention, or 
performance data exists.
